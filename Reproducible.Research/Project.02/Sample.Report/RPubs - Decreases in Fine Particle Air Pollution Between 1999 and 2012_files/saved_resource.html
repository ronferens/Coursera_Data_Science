                    Pablo Adames farajallah • 8 months ago

                    To calculate the month where the most number of negative values are computed I first filtered the non NA and negative dates, then applied the same procedure used here:
                    > negative.nonNA.dates <- pm1$Date[(pm1$Sample.Value<0)& !is.na(pm1$Sample.Value)]
                    > negative.nonNA.dateVec <- as.Date(as.character(negative.nonNA.dates),"%Y%m%d")
                    > negative.nonNA.factorVec <- as.factor(month.name[as.POSIXlt(negative.nonNA.dateVec)$mon + 1 ])
                    > negative.nonNA.table=table(negative.nonNA.factorVec)

                    Then executing the following line gives the target values:

                    > result<-as.data.frame(round(100*negative.nonNA.table/sum(negative.nonNA.table)))
                    > result$monthNum <- unlist(Map(function(x) {match(x,month.name)},result$negative.nonNA.factorVec))
                    > d<-data.frame(result[order(result$monthNum, decreasing=F),],row.names=NULL)
                    > names(d)<-c("month","NegFrac","monthNum")
                    > d$monthNum<-NULL
                    > names(d)<-c("month","NegFrac")
                    > d
                    month NegFrac
                    1 January 7
                    2 February 8
                    3 March 10
                    4 April 7
                    5 May 5
                    6 June 8
                    7 July 9
                    8 August 8
                    9 September 7
                    10 October 10
                    11 November 8
                    12 December 13
                    So apparently the fraction of negative values is higher in the Fall and Winter months. This is slightly different to what Dr. Peng shows here.
